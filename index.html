Warholesque Paintings
Silkscreen ink, stencil, and synthetic polymer paint on canvas

Ron English pays homage to Andy Warhol by pushing Warholian ideas to their logical next step—logical as defined by Ron English.

Employing Warhol-associated materials, techniques, and production methods—including silkscreen and stencil processes, as well as collaboration with master printers such as Donald Sheridan and Alexander Heinrici—English transforms iconic Warhol imagery into something unmistakably his own.

These works include Cowgirls with exaggerated udders, a half-zipped Dandy Banana, Young Andy portraits, obese Ronald McDonalds (street name: MC Supersized), three-eyed Elvis figures, a Cowgirl Guernica diorama, and one of English’s most recognizable images: Marilyn Monroe with Mickey Mouse breasts.

Other Warhol-inspired works include Bruce Lee Action Actor, Figment (Dead Andy), Super Sunflower, and Abraham Obama (Barack Obama fused with Abraham Lincoln).

Paintings in the Warholesque theme are produced through a hybrid process that may involve silkscreen, stencil application, or both, combined with extensive hand-painting in synthetic polymer paint. Each work is finished in unique color combinations. No two are alike.

Process (typical workflow)

  1. Ron paints an original work, usually monotone and without a background.
  2. A high-resolution image is used to prepare a silkscreen and/or stencil.
  3. Canvases are cut and gessoed.
  4. The initial screen or stencil layer is applied as a guide.
  5. Ron paints each canvas in unique color combinations, using the applied image as a reference. Once completed, the guide layer is often no longer visible.
  6. Additional screen or stencil layers may be applied.
  7. The canvases are stretched.

The result is a unique Ron English painting that adopts Warholian production logic while asserting English’s own visual language and conceptual framework.
